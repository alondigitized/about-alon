Retail Intelligence Pulse

Wednesday, May 06 2026 · Footwear, digital commerce, marketplaces

What newly matters now

The clearest overnight change is not another vague AI shopping claim. Amazon formally opened its end-to-end logistics stack to any business, including orders sold off Amazon, which expands platform dependence well beyond marketplace traffic. At the same time, Reuters surfaced harder evidence that Nike is still losing ground in performance footwear while adidas is converting innovation wins into visible market momentum. For a footwear digital leader, the live issue is not just demand creation. It is who controls the infrastructure and who still has product heat strong enough to resist it.

Amazon is pushing from marketplace power into full-stack merchant operating infrastructure

The new control point is below the digital shelf

Amazon said on May 4 that Amazon Supply Chain Services now opens its freight, distribution, fulfillment, and parcel shipping capabilities to businesses of all sizes, including retail, wholesale, and commercial companies, using the same logistics network that powers Amazon.com.

Amazon Supply Chain Services announcement

This matters more than incremental storefront features. Amazon is making itself easier to adopt as the infrastructure layer even when the transaction happens on a brand site or another channel. For footwear brands, that could improve speed and inventory flow, but it also deepens reliance on the same company that already shapes marketplace discovery, pricing pressure, and fulfillment expectations.

Map which capabilities are truly strategic to own versus rentable from a platform. If your team treats marketplace strategy and logistics strategy as separate decisions, that is now outdated.

Nike's turnaround is still losing the market-share race while adidas is winning the innovation headline

Brand power still decides who can bargain with the channel layer

Reuters reported on May 5 that Nike's share of the global sports footwear market fell to 22.9% in 2025 from 25.9% a year earlier, citing Euromonitor data obtained by Reuters. The same report said adidas' share rose to 12.2% from 11.7%, and noted that an adidas-sponsored runner broke the two-hour marathon barrier last week. World Athletics and adidas separately documented Sabastian Sawe's 1:59:30 London Marathon world record in the Adizero Adios Pro Evo 3 on April 26.

adidas performance announcement

The strategic read is blunt. Nike's turnaround is still operational, while adidas currently has both the product-story momentum and the proof point the market notices. That changes channel leverage. Retailers, marketplaces, and consumers are all more forgiving when a brand has innovation heat. When it does not, logistics and distribution efficiency become defensive tools rather than sources of advantage.

Pressure-test whether your next 12 months of digital investment are over-weighted toward experience optimization and under-weighted toward product storytelling, launch velocity, and franchise newness. Strong conversion infrastructure cannot rescue weak product energy for long.

Evidence caveat: the Nike and adidas share-shift framing relies partly on Reuters reporting that cites Euromonitor data obtained by Reuters, not a company filing. The marathon result is independently supported by World Athletics and adidas. I did not find a stronger fresh development from New Balance, Puma, Skechers, On, Hoka, Deckers, Crocs, ASICS, Brooks, Walmart, Target, Lululemon, Macy's, or Nordstrom overnight that changed the strategic picture more than these two points.
