Retail Intelligence Pulse

Monday, June 01 2026 · Footwear, digital commerce, marketplaces

What newly matters now

The strongest signal since yesterday is not a single brand launch. It is the growing evidence that retail winners are stacking three capabilities at once: membership economics, broader digital assortment, and faster fulfillment windows. Walmart and Target are showing that this bundle can materially change shopping behavior, while On and Deckers show premium performance footwear still has room to grow across both owned and partner channels when brand heat and inventory discipline are intact.

Walmart is turning speed from a convenience perk into habitual behavior

The new fact is not just one million drone drops. It is how fast the usage curve is bending.

Walmart said on May 29 that it has completed more than one million drone deliveries, and that slightly over 40% of those deliveries happened in fiscal Q1 2027 alone.

Walmart drone delivery milestone, May 29 2026

Walmart also said drone delivery now operates from 66 stores across four states and five metro markets, with an average delivery time of 23 minutes.

Walmart drone delivery milestone, May 29 2026

In its May 21 Q1 FY27 materials, Walmart said global advertising grew 36%, Walmart Connect in the U.S. grew 44%, and marketplace sales were up nearly 29%.

Walmart Q1 FY27 earnings presentation, May 21 2026

This is what an ecosystem flywheel looks like when it starts compounding. Walmart is not just getting better at delivery. It is converting delivery density into more marketplace relevance, more ad inventory, and more reasons for customers and sellers to stay inside its stack. That matters to footwear brands because the economics of discovery and conversion are moving closer to retail media plus fulfillment infrastructure, not just shelf placement or search rank.

Pressure-test where urgent or replenishment footwear missions could be captured by marketplace-led convenience ecosystems, especially kids, basics, accessories, and weather-driven purchases. If those missions matter, retail media and local availability accuracy deserve more attention than they usually get in footwear planning.

Target is proving that loyalty, same-day delivery, and marketplace can move together

This quarter matters because the growth came from a connected model, not a one-off promotion.

Target said on May 20 that first-quarter net sales rose 6.7% and comparable sales rose 5.6%.

Target Q1 2026 earnings release, May 20 2026

Target said digital comparable sales grew 8.9%, led by more than 27% growth in same-day delivery powered by Target Circle 360.

Target Q1 2026 earnings release, May 20 2026

Target also said non-merchandise sales grew nearly 25%, reflecting strong growth in Roundel ad revenue, Target Circle 360 membership revenue, and the Target+ marketplace.

Target Q1 2026 earnings release, May 20 2026

Target is quietly assembling the same architecture that is powering Walmart, just at a different scale: loyalty-led fulfillment, marketplace expansion, and monetizable media. For brands, the message is that partner economics are getting more layered. A retailer relationship is no longer just wholesale sell-in plus on-site search. It is increasingly membership visibility, same-day eligibility, retail media participation, and marketplace assortment logic all at once.

Revisit the Target account plan through a systems lens. Specifically map which assortments should be same-day eligible, which deserve marketplace breadth, and where Roundel support is doing more than traffic generation by improving the economics of digital sell-through.

On is still showing that premium running can scale in both DTC and wholesale

The useful signal is balance, not just top-line growth.

On said on May 12 that first-quarter 2026 net sales rose 14.5% to CHF 831.9 million, or 26.4% on a constant-currency basis.

On Q1 2026 results, May 12 2026

On said direct-to-consumer sales increased 16.4% to CHF 322.3 million, while wholesale sales increased 13.3% to CHF 509.6 million.

On Q1 2026 results, May 12 2026

That mix matters. On is not growing because wholesale is weak and DTC is masking it, or vice versa. It is expanding in both channels at the same time, which usually signals real consumer demand, healthy partner appetite, and disciplined product allocation. For incumbent footwear players, this is a reminder that premium positioning still works when innovation, distribution discipline, and brand storytelling stay aligned.

Benchmark current running and performance strategies against On's channel balance. If one channel is structurally over-relied on, treat that as a strategic risk rather than a budgeting issue.

Deckers keeps reinforcing that HOKA growth is surviving scale

The strategic takeaway is that the performance-running demand pool is still deep.

Deckers said on May 21 that fiscal 2026 revenue rose 9.8% to $5.47 billion, while HOKA net sales increased 16.0% to $2.59 billion for the year.

Deckers fiscal 2026 results, May 21 2026

For the fourth quarter, Deckers said direct-to-consumer net sales increased 13.2% to $464.4 million and wholesale net sales increased 7.4% to $654.9 million.

Deckers fiscal 2026 results, May 21 2026

HOKA's momentum matters beyond Deckers because it confirms that premium technical footwear demand is not exhausted, even as the category gets more crowded. The bigger lesson is organizational: scale does not have to force a brand into blunt discounting or wholesale dependency if product cadence, demand shaping, and channel segmentation are handled well.

Use HOKA and On as the current benchmark set for premium running execution. Focus less on headline growth envy and more on the mechanics underneath it: launch pacing, full-price discipline, and channel role clarity.

Evidence quality is strong overall because all core facts above come from company-issued earnings materials or official corporate announcements. The weakest element is interpretation around what these patterns mean for footwear channel strategy, which is my inference from the reported results rather than a direct company claim.
