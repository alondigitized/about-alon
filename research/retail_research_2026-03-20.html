Retail Intelligence Pulse

Date: 2026-03-20 · Lens: footwear, digital commerce, marketplaces, loyalty, merchandising, omnichannel, operating model

1. Executive summary

Bottom line: There does not appear to be a major fresh disclosure overnight from the primary footwear set. The highest-signal developments that newly matter now are: (1) Lululemon guiding to softer 2026 growth while explicitly prioritizing SKU reduction, inventory rebalancing, and a return to full-price selling in North America; (2) Macy’s showing measurable improvement in comparable sales while still guiding cautiously and extending store-closure timing, a useful read-through on department-store demand and off-price/wholesale risk; and (3) a new European channel read indicating lifestyle sneaker demand remains soft, promotions are heavy, and shelf space ceded by Nike has been captured by Hoka, On, New Balance, and Puma.

For a footwear digital leader, the implication is straightforward: the market is still rewarding freshness, disciplined inventory, and balanced channel execution more than brute-force traffic generation or discounting.

2. What changed today

  • No major new overnight disclosure from Nike, Adidas, New Balance, Puma, Skechers, On, Hoka/Deckers, Crocs, ASICS, or Brooks found in high-signal sources during this sweep.
  • What newly matters now is the accumulation of the last 48-72 hours: Lululemon’s guidance reset and full-price discipline plan; Macy’s positive comp but cautious 2026 stance; and a fresh Europe wholesale/channel datapoint showing continuing pressure in lifestyle footwear.

3. Source-backed facts by company or theme

Lululemon

  • Reuters reported on March 17 that Lululemon forecast 2026 revenue of $11.35B-$11.50B and EPS of $12.10-$12.30, both below analyst expectations.
  • Lululemon said a top priority is returning to full-price sales growth in North America through product newness, SKU reduction, and inventory rebalancing.
  • Reuters also reported Q4 gross margin was down 550 bps, including a 520 bps tariff impact, and the company sees roughly $380M in annual tariff impact.
  • The company also appointed former Levi Strauss CEO Chip Bergh to the board amid leadership transition and proxy pressure.

Macy’s

  • CNBC reported on March 18 that Macy’s beat Q4 expectations with $7.64B revenue and $1.67 adjusted EPS.
  • Macy’s guided FY2026 revenue to $21.4B-$21.65B and comparable sales to -0.5% to +0.5%, reflecting a cautious stance despite recent progress.
  • Comparable sales across the company grew 1.8% in Q4 including owned/licensed merchandise and its third-party marketplace.
  • Macy’s management said the business returned to positive annual comparable-sales growth for the first time in three years, but extended timing for remaining store closures through 2028 rather than rushing the asset exits.
  • Management flagged tariff pressure as most meaningful in Q1.

Europe lifestyle footwear / Nike-Adidas-emerging brands

  • WWD reported on March 18, citing BNP Paribas research and commentary from Sport 2000’s commercial leadership, that lifestyle footwear represented 18%-19% of 2025 sales at Sport 2000 versus 25% in 2024.
  • The same report said elevated inventory and heavy promotions are expected to continue in the first half of 2026.
  • According to the report, Nike’s prior wholesale pullback still appears to be affecting retailer relationships and shelf space allocation; the space has been picked up by Hoka, Puma, New Balance, and On.
  • The report also said Adidas is seeing a material slowdown in lifestyle offerings, consistent with broader softness in the category.
  • WWD referenced Adidas’ March 4 commentary that 2026 growth should continue but slow to high-single digits.

Nike digital/wholesale context (not new today, but relevant context)

  • Digital Commerce 360 reported that Nike’s fiscal Q2 2026 digital sales fell 14% year over year, the eighth consecutive quarterly decline, while wholesale grew and North America showed progress with fewer promotions and stronger full-price demand.
  • That matters because the current Europe channel softness suggests the recovery path may remain uneven by geography and by channel even if wholesale relationships are improving.

4. Why it matters for footwear digital

  • Full-price discipline is back at the center. Lululemon is explicitly using SKU rationalization and inventory rebalancing to restore healthier selling. That is not a marketing message; it is margin triage.
  • Department-store traffic is not dead, but it is fragile. Macy’s results suggest better execution can still produce comp improvement, yet management is not confident enough to call a broad rebound. That matters for wholesale exposure, promotional planning, and partner inventory commitments.
  • Wholesale shelf space is sticky once lost. The Europe read-through reinforces that stepping back from wholesale can create openings that competitors keep, especially when the category is promotional and consumers are shifting away from classic lifestyle sneaker demand.
  • Digital growth without merchandise productivity is hollow. Nike’s digital declines and Lululemon’s SKU reset point to the same lesson: search/personalization/CRM can help, but they do not fix stale assortments or overbought inventory.

5. Strategic implications

  1. Assortment sharpness now matters more than broad depth. Fewer, clearer winners are outperforming broad long-tail catalogs in pressured categories.
  2. Channel strategy should be measured by share retention, not just margin math. Protecting wholesale presence in strategic accounts may be worth some near-term economic trade-off if it preserves discoverability and shelf authority.
  3. Promotion risk remains high through H1. Europe in particular still looks promotional, which raises the bar for markdown governance, regional pricing discipline, and inventory visibility.

6. Recommended actions

  1. Run a “full-price resilience” review by category and region: identify where conversion is being bought with promo rather than earned with product, and which SKUs should be cut, held, or protected.
  2. Pressure-test key wholesale accounts for shelf-space and digital-placement risk, especially where prior DTC-first decisions may have weakened presence.
  3. Audit lifestyle vs. performance mix in Europe and North America: if lifestyle demand is softening while performance challengers are taking share, merchandising and media should lean harder into performance credibility and newness.
