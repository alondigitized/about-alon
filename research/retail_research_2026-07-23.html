Retail Intelligence Pulse

Thursday, July 23 2026 · Footwear, digital commerce, marketplaces

What newly matters now

Three signals are lining up. First, PUMA just created a dedicated Vice President of E-Commerce role, which is an operating-model tell: e-commerce is no longer being treated as a sub-function inside broader DTC. Second, Target is moving discovery, loyalty linking, and checkout into conversational AI surfaces, while also making curated marketplace inventory more visible there. Third, Amazon keeps training customers to delegate shopping tasks to AI, not just search through catalogs. For footwear and apparel brands, the control point is shifting from the homepage to the machine-readable shelf: product data, official assortment, loyalty portability, and marketplace discipline.

Best near-term response: tighten product-data quality for AI-mediated discovery, decide where official digital storefront control matters more than broad distribution, and make sure loyalty benefits can survive when the shopping journey starts outside your owned app.

PUMA just made e-commerce a first-class leadership seat

On July 22, PUMA appointed Dusan Hamlin to the newly created role of Vice President E-Commerce with immediate effect, reporting to Chief Commercial Officer Matthias Bäumer.

PUMA said Hamlin will drive the global strategy and commercial direction of its digital and e-commerce businesses across its website, apps and marketplaces.

The company said this follows its late-2025 decision to split direct-to-consumer into two dedicated areas: Global Retail and Global E-Commerce.

In PUMA's Q1 2026 results, the company said e-commerce sales rose 0.6% currency-adjusted, supported by additional e-commerce marketplaces across APAC.

This is the real signal, not the title itself: PUMA is formalizing e-commerce as a distinct operating system with its own leadership, capabilities and roadmap. That usually precedes harder decisions on platform architecture, marketplace management, performance marketing, and how much autonomy digital teams get versus regional or retail organizations.

Benchmark whether your own digital org still treats e-commerce as a reporting layer inside DTC instead of as a distinct capability stack. If it does, expect slower decisions around marketplaces, content, pricing, and AI commerce readiness.

Target is pushing loyalty-linked shopping into AI channels, not just its own site

Target said on June 18 that AI-driven traffic to Target jumped 2,000% in Q1 year over year, versus nearly 400% growth for retail sites overall, citing Adobe data for the broader benchmark.

Target said shoppers can now browse and buy Target items in Google Search including AI Mode and the Gemini app, use loyalty-linked checkout inside Microsoft Copilot, and shop a full Target app experience inside ChatGPT with multi-item baskets plus Drive Up, Order Pickup or shipping.

On July 1, Target said it is adding dozens of hand-picked brands to Target Plus and making those items show up more clearly in AI-powered search and digital discovery experiences through partners including OpenAI, Google and Microsoft.

Target is not treating conversational AI as top-of-funnel experimentation. It is wiring discovery, basket building, fulfillment choice and loyalty benefits into external AI interfaces. That matters because it lowers the odds that retailer apps remain the default starting point for product discovery, especially in replenishment and consideration-driven categories.

Pressure-test whether your loyalty value proposition still works when the journey starts in ChatGPT, Gemini or Copilot instead of your app. If benefits, identity and fulfillment options cannot travel across those surfaces, loyalty will weaken exactly where new traffic is forming.

Amazon keeps moving from AI-assisted search to AI-executed commerce

Amazon said more than 250 million customers have used Rufus this year, with monthly users up 140% year over year and interactions up 210% year over year.

Amazon said customers who use Rufus during a shopping journey are more than 60% more likely to convert.

Amazon said products available through Buy for Me grew from 65,000 at launch to more than 500,000, extending shopping beyond Amazon's own first-party catalog.

In its latest Prime Day shopping explainer, Amazon highlighted newer behaviors it is encouraging customers to adopt: personalized deal guides, deal alerts based on natural-language needs, price-triggered alerts, and automatic purchase at a target price.

Amazon is normalizing delegated shopping behavior: not just asking AI what to buy, but letting AI monitor, compare, and transact. For brands, that means clean PDP data, availability, sizing confidence, and price integrity become even more important than persuasive creative, because the AI layer increasingly decides which options even reach the shortlist.

Treat AI-readable product data as conversion infrastructure. For footwear, prioritize fit signals, model-level differentiation, inventory accuracy, and official brand presentation in any channel where an AI layer might intermediate selection.

Source quality is strongest on the PUMA leadership move because it is a direct company announcement and is supported by PUMA's own recent channel-performance disclosure. The Target and Amazon points are also direct company statements, but several of the traffic, usage and conversion metrics are company-reported rather than independently audited. The conclusion that the control point is moving toward AI-mediated discovery and transaction is interpretation from those facts.
