Retail Intelligence Pulse

Prepared for Alan • Footwear / digital leadership lens • March 19, 2026 (America/Los_Angeles)

1. Executive summary

Today’s signal is narrower but still useful. The most material updates were not from Nike/Adidas/Deckers/Crocs themselves, but from adjacent retail and athletic-apparel players that shape the operating environment for footwear digital leaders. The strongest hard facts this morning are: (1) Macy’s reported better-than-expected Q4 results but guided cautiously for 2026, explicitly citing tariffs and pressure on discretionary demand; (2) Lululemon guided below expectations and is now dealing with a governance fight and unresolved CEO succession at the same time it is trying to restore full-price growth in North America; and (3) Amazon’s ecosystem remains strategically unstable in ways that matter for marketplace and search/discovery strategy, with Prime Day reportedly shifting earlier and a court temporarily allowing Perplexity’s AI shopping agents to continue operating on Amazon.

For a footwear digital leader, the read-through is not “retail is weak” in the abstract. It is more specific: discretionary demand remains uneven, margin recovery is increasingly tied to inventory discipline and reduced markdowns, and AI-mediated commerce is moving from hype to channel-control conflict. Also notable: there were no major overnight source-backed announcements from the primary footwear brand set that changed the thesis materially.

2. What changed today

  • Macy’s: Reported Q4/FY2025 results and issued cautious FY2026 guidance, pointing to macro/geopolitical uncertainty and tariff pressure, with first-half tariff impact expected to be more meaningful.
  • Lululemon: Its weak 2026 outlook became a broader strategic governance story on March 18 as founder Chip Wilson publicly pushed again for a substantial board refresh after the company announced a new board appointment and director exit.
  • No major new primary-footwear-company disclosures detected overnight: No source-backed announcement from Nike, Adidas, Deckers/Hoka, Crocs, Skechers, Puma, ASICS, Brooks, On, or New Balance materially changed the picture since yesterday.

3. Source-backed facts by company or theme

Macy’s: better quarter, cautious year

  • CNBC reported Macy’s beat Wall Street’s quarterly sales and profit expectations, while guiding FY2026 revenue to $21.4B-$21.65B and adjusted EPS to $1.90-$2.10, both down versus FY2025.
  • CEO Tony Spring said the company is taking a “prudent” view because of uncertainty around gas prices, geopolitical conflict, consumer resilience, and tariffs.
  • CNBC reported Macy’s expects tariff impact to be heavier in the first half, with Q1 the most meaningful period.
  • Comparable sales outlook for FY2026 was guided to roughly flat: down 0.5% to up 0.5%.

Interpretation: Hard evidence of a still-cautious discretionary environment, even when execution improves.

Lululemon: growth pressure + governance pressure

  • Reuters reported Lululemon forecast FY2026 revenue of $11.35B-$11.50B versus analyst expectations of $11.52B, and EPS of $12.10-$12.30 versus expectations of $12.58.
  • Reuters reported Lululemon said it expects about $380M of annual U.S. tariff impact, up from $275M in 2025.
  • Q4 gross margin was down 550 basis points, including 520 bps from U.S. import tariffs, according to Reuters.
  • Management said a top priority is returning to full-price sales growth in North America via product newness, SKU reduction, and rebalanced inventory.
  • On March 18, Reuters reported founder Chip Wilson said a “substantial” board refresh is still needed, despite director David Mussafer’s planned exit and Chip Bergh’s addition to the board.
  • Reuters also noted the company is still awaiting a permanent CEO.

Interpretation: This is not just an apparel earnings miss; it is a clean example of how brand heat, inventory architecture, pricing power, and governance instability can all compound at once.

Amazon retail calendar: Prime Day may move earlier

  • Reuters, citing Bloomberg, reported Amazon plans to move Prime Day from July to late June.
  • Reuters said last year’s event drove $24.1B in U.S. online spending across retailers, up 30% year over year, according to Adobe Analytics.
  • Reuters also highlighted that Walmart has been gaining in digital fulfillment, with e-commerce contribution to U.S. sales almost doubling in its most recent quarter and under-three-hour delivery users growing more than 60% in fiscal 2026.

Interpretation: If the report proves accurate, promotional cadence and traffic capture could pull forward earlier into late Q2.

AI shopping / marketplace control: Amazon vs. Perplexity

  • Reuters reported the 9th U.S. Circuit temporarily paused an order that had blocked Perplexity AI from using its agentic shopping tool on Amazon.
  • Amazon’s lawsuit alleges covert access to customer accounts and disguised automated activity; Perplexity argues consumers should be able to choose their AI tools.

Interpretation: A concrete sign that AI agents are now a platform-governance and traffic-control issue, not just a feature discussion.

Target: price-led turnaround + store network segmentation

  • Reuters reported Target’s new CEO cut prices on more than 3,000 products by 5%-20%.
  • Reuters said the company’s 2026 growth plan includes a $6B budget, faster restocking, speedier deliveries, greater AI focus, and a shift toward differentiating some stores as fulfillment-heavy hubs while others focus on shopper experience.

Interpretation: Another retailer is explicitly pairing sharper value signaling with operating-model changes in stores and fulfillment.

New Balance: resale expansion is strategically relevant, but not an overnight change

  • Digital Commerce 360 reported New Balance expanded its Reconsidered resale program from footwear into apparel.
  • The report said the program has recirculated more than 100,000 pairs of shoes since launch and now supports trade-in by mail or at 100+ retail locations for accepted footwear.

Interpretation: Useful signal on how premium footwear brands can use owned resale as acquisition, retention, and margin-protection infrastructure. However, this item is older than today’s core developments and should be treated as secondary.

4. Why it matters for footwear digital

  • Full-price selling is still fragile. Lululemon’s explicit focus on product newness, SKU reduction, and inventory rebalancing is the clearest current example of what it takes to restore full-price growth when a premium brand loses some heat.
  • Tariff pressure is still distorting margin math. Macy’s and Lululemon both cited tariffs directly. That matters for footwear because sourcing concentration and seasonality make promotional mistakes more expensive.
  • Retail calendar timing may shift. An earlier Prime Day would compress demand capture, media planning, and promo decisions into late Q2.
  • AI shopping is becoming a channel-power battle. If agents mediate search and checkout, brands risk losing visibility, owned audience access, and merchandising control unless they define a position now.
  • Store networks are being re-architected for omnichannel economics. Target’s hub-vs.-shopper-store segmentation is relevant to footwear brands balancing service, ship-from-store, BOPIS, and labor productivity.

5. Strategic implications

  1. The next margin battleground is not just cost; it is assortment discipline. The Lululemon situation reinforces that SKU sprawl, stale product flow, and delayed inventory correction can force markdown dependence even before demand fully cracks.
  2. Marketplace and AI-agent strategy needs executive ownership now. Amazon/Perplexity shows that agentic commerce may sit in legal gray zones for a while, but brands still need a position on discoverability, content control, identity, and whether they want to enable or resist these intermediaries.
  3. Wholesale partners remain strategically important when discretionary demand is uneven. Macy’s caution and Target’s aggressive operating reset both suggest that brands should not assume a clean DTC-led demand rebound; strong wholesale execution and cleaner account segmentation still matter.

6. Recommended actions

1. Re-check your late-Q2 / early-Q3 promotional calendar this week. Build a contingency plan for an earlier Amazon traffic spike and competitor promo pull-forward.
2. Ask your team for a one-page readout on full-price sell-through risk by key footwear franchises: newness cadence, SKU count changes, planned markdown exposure, and top inventory imbalances.
3. Define an explicit AI-commerce stance before the channel gets intermediated around you: what product data can agents consume, what commerce journeys you will support, and where you need identity or loyalty protections.
