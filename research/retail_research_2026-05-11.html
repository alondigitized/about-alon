Retail Intelligence Pulse

Monday, May 11 2026 · Footwear, digital commerce, marketplaces

What newly matters now

There was no major new primary-footwear-company disclosure overnight. The sharper read is structural. Amazon is now connecting homepage personalization, AI buying assistance, and off-Amazon merchant storefronts into one demand-capture stack. Walmart is taking the opposite but equally serious stance, pushing hard on measurable enterprise AI and execution discipline. In footwear, that raises the stakes for On's May 12 earnings because performance running is still one of the few areas showing clean momentum rather than broad category recovery.

Amazon is collapsing discovery, decisioning, and merchant demand capture into one surface

The platform is getting stronger before the shopper ever reaches a brand site

Amazon said on May 8 that it introduced a new homepage experience with more personalized recommendations, more visibly merchandised deals and seasonal events, and a redesigned navigation meant to make browsing easier. Amazon also said shoppers in the U.S. can now use its "Interests" feature and AI shopping guides to surface products in more tailored ways.

Amazon homepage and discovery update

Amazon said on May 8 that it is testing "Buy for Me," an agentic shopping feature that lets some U.S. customers buy products from other brand sites inside the Amazon Shopping app when Amazon does not sell the item directly.

Amazon Buy for Me test

This is not generic AI theater. Amazon is trying to own three layers at once: discovery, shopping advice, and transaction routing, even when the final merchant is outside Amazon's own first-party catalog. For footwear brands, that threatens to weaken the distinction between marketplace traffic and DTC traffic if Amazon becomes the default starting point either way.

Pressure-test whether your product data, pricing, and brand presentation stay coherent when discovery starts on Amazon but fulfillment or checkout may route elsewhere. If they do not, your DTC economics can erode without an obvious topline warning.

Walmart is framing AI as an operating system question, not a novelty feature

Management is explicitly tying AI deployment to measurable enterprise value

In Walmart's fiscal 2026 annual report filed on April 30, the company said it is using AI to improve search, recommendations, and operational workflows, but also said it aims to deploy AI only where it drives measurable value for customers, members, associates, and the business.

Walmart fiscal 2026 annual report

Walmart also reiterated that eCommerce was profitable globally in both Q4 and for the full year, and that store-fulfilled pickup and delivery continue to be central to its customer proposition.

Walmart Q4 and FY2026 earnings release

Amazon is trying to become the interface. Walmart is trying to become the most efficient retail machine. Both matter, but they imply different competitive responses. For a footwear brand selling through wholesale and DTC, Walmart's posture is a reminder that AI only matters when it improves economics, service levels, or conversion at scale.

Separate AI experiments into two buckets: shopper-facing conversion gains and operating-cost reductions. If a use case does not clearly hit one of those, it is probably noise.

Performance running remains the cleanest live read in footwear

On's May 12 report matters because recent data still shows momentum concentrating in specialist winners

Brooks said on May 1 that it delivered 23% global growth in the first quarter, including 20% growth in North America, 30% currency-neutral growth in EMEA, and 136% growth in China. Brooks also said it remained the number-one brand in U.S. specialty running footwear.

Brooks Q1 2026 update

On said it will report first quarter 2026 results on Tuesday, May 12, 2026 before U.S. markets open.

On Q1 2026 earnings date

The market is still handing out growth selectively. If On also posts healthy momentum, it strengthens the case that performance running remains one of the few parts of footwear where product credibility and execution can still overwhelm broader category softness. That is a direct competitive problem for Nike, adidas, and any scaled player assuming demand will normalize back to incumbents.

Read On's results for channel mix, inventory discipline, and regional demand quality, not just growth rate. If premium performance brands are still clean on those metrics, the category reset is not close to over.

Evidence caveat: Amazon, Walmart, Brooks, and On links above are company-issued materials, so the factual claims about launches, timing, and reported metrics are reasonably strong. The competitive read is interpretation. Walmart's annual-report language is broad by design, so it signals direction more than near-term magnitude. Brooks' release is not a full SEC-style earnings package, which limits comparability.
