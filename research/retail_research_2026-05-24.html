Retail Intelligence Pulse

Sunday, May 24 2026 · Footwear, digital commerce, marketplaces

What newly matters now

The most useful read this morning is not another generic AI headline. It is the combination of three harder signals. Amazon is pushing AI shopping into the main demand path and extending it off Amazon. Walmart is showing that membership and advertising are not side businesses, but economic support beams that help fund price leadership and ecommerce scale. Deckers, meanwhile, just put out a multi-year framework that says premium footwear growth can continue without obvious channel or margin compromise. For a footwear leader, the implication is simple: the battleground is shifting toward who controls discovery, who subsidizes conversion, and who can preserve pricing power while still scaling.

Amazon is making AI shopping the interface, not the feature

Its assistant is now embedded in search and can route demand beyond Amazon's own inventory

Amazon said on May 13 that Rufus was renamed Alexa for Shopping, and that more than 250 million customers have used Rufus this year, with monthly users up 140 percent year over year and interactions up 210 percent year over year.

Amazon: generative and agentic AI shopping update

Amazon also said customers who use Rufus during a shopping journey are more than 60 percent more likely to convert.

Amazon: generative and agentic AI shopping update

The company said products available through Buy for Me, which can purchase items from brand websites when they are not sold in Amazon's store, increased from 65,000 at launch to more than 500,000.

Amazon: generative and agentic AI shopping update

This is strategically bigger than a chatbot refresh. Amazon is trying to own the recommendation layer, the comparison layer, and in some cases the transaction layer even when inventory sits off-platform. That compresses the distance between brand demand creation and Amazon-mediated conversion.

Pressure-test footwear content, pricing, and inventory readiness for a world where AI assistants decide which products get surfaced first. If Amazon can intermediate even off-Amazon purchases, digital shelf strategy can no longer stop at your own site and key wholesale PDPs.

Walmart is showing how to fund digital growth without giving up the value position

Advertising, membership, and store-based ecommerce are compounding together

Reuters reported on May 19 that Walmart's advertising business and membership fees together accounted for an estimated 27 percent of operating profit last fiscal year, up from 9 percent in 2021, citing Morningstar data.

Reuters: how Walmart won the year of the tariffs

The same Reuters report said Walmart's ecommerce sales grew 24 percent last fiscal year to $150.4 billion, reaching 21.3 percent of total net sales, up from 17.9 percent a year earlier.

Reuters: how Walmart won the year of the tariffs

Walmart said in its May 21 earnings release that global ecommerce sales grew 26 percent in Q1 FY27, global advertising grew 37 percent, and membership income rose 17.4 percent.

Walmart: Q1 FY27 earnings

Walmart's model matters because it shows how digital profit pools can subsidize sharper price perception and faster fulfillment at the same time. For brands that depend on wholesale, that means the most dangerous partner is no longer just the one with scale. It is the one whose ecosystem lets it reinvest higher-margin revenue streams back into shopper value and convenience.

Re-rank wholesale partners by economic flywheel strength, not just door count or traffic. If a partner can blend media, membership, and local fulfillment, it may deserve differentiated launches, exclusive colorways, or tighter inventory synchronization.

Deckers just made a multi-year case for disciplined premium footwear growth

HOKA is being managed as a durable growth engine, not a short-cycle hot streak

Deckers said on May 21 that it expects fiscal 2027 net sales of $5.86 billion to $5.91 billion, with HOKA expected to grow by a low-double-digit percentage and operating margin around 21.5 percent.

Deckers: Q4 FY2026 results and outlook

Deckers also provided a fiscal 2028 through 2030 framework calling for high-single-digit annual consolidated sales growth, low-double-digit HOKA growth, and operating margin maintained in the low 20-plus percent range.

Deckers: Q4 FY2026 results and outlook

The company said inventories, including the impact of incremental tariffs, were $487.0 million at March 31, 2026 versus $495.2 million a year earlier.

Deckers: Q4 FY2026 results and outlook

That is one of the strongest current footwear signals in market because management is not just defending a quarter. It is telling investors that premium positioning, brand heat, and channel discipline are durable enough to support multi-year growth with margin resilience, even with tariffs still in the background.

Benchmark your own footwear business against this standard. If long-range planning still assumes growth must come with lower gross margin, broader wholesale exposure, or more promotions, the problem may be product-market strength and channel design, not just macro pressure.

Evidence caveat: Amazon's usage and conversion statistics are self-reported by the company, so they are strongest as indicators of Amazon's strategic direction rather than neutral industry measurement. Reuters' Walmart piece adds useful context and third-party interpretation, but some figures there rely on Morningstar estimates rather than company disclosure. Deckers' outlook is first-hand management guidance, which is credible for intent but still forward-looking.
