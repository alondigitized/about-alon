Retail Intelligence Pulse

Wednesday, June 03 2026 · Footwear, digital commerce, marketplaces

What newly matters now

Three signals are getting harder to dismiss. Amazon now says AI-guided shopping is not just a demo layer but a measurable conversion lever. Walmart is taking friction out of its first mile by collapsing prepaid freight flows into a single managed network. And premium performance footwear players HOKA and On are still growing fast without leaning on obvious discount-driven demand. For a footwear digital leader, that points to a sharper near-term agenda: make product data machine-readable, make inventory flow more controllable, and protect full-price demand where brand heat still gives you leverage.

Amazon says AI shopping is already changing conversion economics

The meaningful shift is that Amazon is now attaching scale metrics to AI-assisted commerce behavior.

Amazon said on May 29 that more than 250 million customers worldwide have used its AI shopping features this year, including Interests, Rufus, review highlights, and AI Shopping Guides.

Amazon AI shopping update, May 29 2026

Amazon also said customers who interact with AI Shopping Guides and then view recommended products are more than 60% more likely to make a purchase.

Amazon AI shopping update, May 29 2026

If that holds at scale, the strategic issue is no longer whether AI shopping interfaces are real. It is whether branded product data, attributes, reviews, and assortment logic are good enough for those systems to route intent toward your products instead of someone else's. In footwear, the assistant layer can become a silent merchandiser, especially in replenishment, training, running, and gift missions where shoppers accept narrowing help.

Pressure-test product feed quality, attribute completeness, review coverage, and variant logic across Amazon and other high-intent discovery surfaces. Treat agent-readiness like SEO once was: not a lab exercise, but a revenue hygiene issue.

Walmart is attacking supply chain friction upstream, not just promising faster delivery downstream

The sharper signal is network control over inbound inventory, not a customer-facing feature launch.

Walmart said on May 30 that suppliers can now use Walmart-preferred carriers for prepaid freight shipments into all 42 of Walmart's U.S. regional distribution centers through a single managed program with Blue Yonder transportation management software.

Walmart inbound freight program announcement, May 30 2026

Walmart said the program is designed to reduce transportation costs, improve on-time delivery, and simplify inbound execution for suppliers by giving them one point of access to preferred carriers.

Walmart inbound freight program announcement, May 30 2026

This matters because retail speed is usually discussed at the last mile, but margin and in-stock performance are often won or lost earlier. A retailer that standardizes more of the inbound move can reduce variability, tighten appointment compliance, and gain cleaner visibility into where inventory sits before it ever becomes an e-commerce availability promise. That is especially relevant for footwear vendors balancing seasonal launches, replenishment basics, and wholesale service levels.

For key wholesale accounts, map where inbound execution still depends on fragmented carrier choices, weak appointment discipline, or poor ASN data. The opportunity is not abstract efficiency. It is fewer late receipts, cleaner availability, and less markdown risk from avoidable flow misses.

HOKA and On are still proving premium footwear can scale without obvious margin-destructive demand tactics

The important point is the quality of growth, not just the headline revenue rates.

Deckers said on May 22 that HOKA fiscal fourth quarter net sales grew 10.0% to $586.1 million, while direct-to-consumer net sales grew 23.3% and wholesale net sales grew 4.8%.

Deckers / HOKA materials · Deckers fiscal 2026 Q4 results, May 22 2026

On said on May 13 that first quarter 2026 net sales rose 43.0% year over year to CHF 726.6 million, with DTC up 45.3% and wholesale up 41.5%.

On Q1 2026 results, May 13 2026

The signal is that premium performance footwear demand is still supporting both DTC and wholesale at the same time for strong brands. That matters because it argues against the lazy view that growth now requires blunt discounting or channel conflict. The better reading is that differentiated product, focused brand heat, and disciplined distribution can still create room for both margin and scale, even in a noisier promotional market.

Benchmark your footwear portfolio against this pattern by SKU tier, not brand average. Identify where premium positioning is genuinely earning full-price resilience and where weak product storytelling or channel overlap is forcing artificial markdown support.

Evidence quality is strongest in the Walmart, Deckers, and On sections because the core facts come from official company releases.
