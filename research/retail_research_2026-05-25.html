Retail Intelligence Pulse

Monday, May 25 2026 · Footwear, digital commerce, marketplaces

What newly matters now

Three signals matter more than generic retail AI noise this morning. Nike is wiring its own assortment directly into Google’s conversational shopping flow instead of waiting for consumers to land on Nike.com first. Target’s latest quarter shows that loyalty, retail media, and marketplace revenue are scaling fast enough to change the economics of the model, not just pad the edges. And adidas is still growing while staying conservative on wholesale and full-price discipline, which sharpens the contrast with Nike’s still-fragile reset. The common thread is simple: the winners are building stronger control over discovery, margin mix, and inventory discipline at the same time.

Nike is pushing DTC into the AI discovery layer

It wants conversational commerce reach without giving up the customer relationship

Nike said on May 19 that U.S. consumers will be able to discover and purchase Nike products directly from Nike inside the Gemini app and AI Mode in Google Search, with launch planned for early June.

Nike: AI-powered shopping on Google

Nike said the capability is designed to move consumers from product discovery to checkout directly inside Google’s conversational experiences, while Nike maintains the consumer relationship.

Nike: AI-powered shopping on Google

This move comes after Nike reported on March 31 that fiscal third-quarter NIKE Direct revenue fell 4 percent reported and 7 percent currency-neutral, with NIKE Brand Digital down 9 percent.

Nike: fiscal 2026 third quarter results

This is not just a media partnership. It is a structural attempt to recapture qualified demand higher up the funnel, inside AI-native shopping behavior, without surrendering checkout or first-party customer ownership to a marketplace intermediary.

Audit whether your brand can plug into AI-assisted commerce surfaces with clean catalog data, current inventory, and transaction-ready product content. If the interface layer shifts away from branded sites, weak feed quality becomes a growth tax.

Target is proving that loyalty, ads, and marketplace revenue can change the math

Its faster-growing non-merchandise businesses are becoming a real operating lever

Target said on May 20 that first-quarter non-merchandise sales grew 24.6 percent, driven by growth in Roundel advertising, Target Circle 360 membership revenue, and the Target Plus marketplace.

Target: first quarter 2026 earnings

The company said first-quarter comparable sales grew 5.6 percent, with digitally originated comparable sales up 8.9 percent, led by more than 27 percent growth in same-day delivery powered by Target Circle 360.

Target: first quarter 2026 earnings

Target also said gross margin improved to 29.0 percent from 28.2 percent, helped by supply chain productivity, growth in advertising and other non-merchandise revenues, and lower markdown rates.

Target: first quarter 2026 earnings

This is the more important Target story than raw comp growth. The company is showing that loyalty, media, marketplace, and store-based fulfillment can reinforce margin and convenience simultaneously. For brands, that means some retail partners are becoming media, membership, and fulfillment platforms all at once.

Reassess partner strategies through a platform lens. Retailers with growing ad, loyalty, and marketplace economics may deserve deeper data-sharing, better launch sequencing, and tighter inventory coordination because their ability to convert demand is improving faster than their sales mix alone suggests.

adidas is still gaining while Nike is still repairing

Performance share gains look more durable when they hold under full-price and wholesale discipline

adidas said on April 29 that first-quarter 2026 currency-neutral revenue grew 14 percent, footwear revenue rose 4 percent on top of more than 20 percent growth in the prior-year quarter, and performance revenue increased 29 percent, led by football, running, and training.

adidas: first quarter 2026 results

adidas also said it sustained a full-price sales focus across DTC, saw higher gross margins in both ecommerce and own retail, and kept a conservative wholesale sell-in approach in Europe and North America because of promotional market conditions.

adidas: first quarter 2026 results

Reuters reported on May 5 that Nike’s global sports footwear market share fell to 22.9 percent in 2025 from 25.9 percent a year earlier, while adidas rose to 12.2 percent from 11.7 percent, citing Euromonitor data obtained by Reuters.

Reuters via Investing.com: pressure rises on Nike

The meaningful point is not just that adidas is growing. It is that growth is showing up while the company still claims tighter full-price and wholesale discipline. That suggests at least part of the performance-footwear share shift is structural, not just promotional noise.

For any footwear portfolio that still assumes incumbency will automatically reassert itself, tighten competitive monitoring around running innovation, wholesale sell-through, and markdown depth. Share losses become harder to reverse once challenger brands prove they can grow without loosening channel discipline.

Evidence caveat: Nike’s Google commerce announcement is a first-party strategic disclosure, so it is strong on intent and launch design but not yet proof of consumer adoption. Target’s quarter is first-hand company reporting and therefore strong on reported performance, though management framing remains self-interested. The Nike versus adidas share comparison comes from Reuters citing Euromonitor data, which is useful third-party context but still second-hand unless reviewed directly from the underlying dataset.
