Retail Intelligence Pulse

Wednesday, May 27 2026 · Footwear, digital commerce, marketplaces

What newly matters now

The cleanest signal this morning is that digital scale is becoming more about mix quality than raw online growth. Walmart’s latest quarter shows that fast fulfillment, marketplace seller monetization, and membership economics are reinforcing each other. In parallel, Target is publicly acknowledging that usage-based AI pricing is changing deployment math. For a footwear operator, the message is simple: digital ambition without disciplined economics is getting exposed faster.

Walmart is showing what the next profit stack looks like

The important shift is not just e-commerce growth, it is the monetization layer sitting on top of it

Walmart said on May 21 that global eCommerce sales grew 26 percent in Q1 FY27, while global advertising revenue increased 37 percent and Walmart Connect U.S. revenue grew 44 percent excluding VIZIO.

Walmart Releases Q1 FY27 Earnings

Walmart also said marketplace, membership, and advertising are improving business mix, and that roughly 50 percent of Walmart U.S. eCommerce fulfillment center volume is now automated.

Walmart Releases Q1 FY27 Earnings

In its earnings call transcript, Walmart said marketplace sellers increased ad spend by more than 50 percent, Walmart+ net adds reached a new Q1 high, Walmart+ members spend four times more than non-members overall and make seven times more eCommerce visits each year, and more than 36 percent of store-fulfilled delivery orders in Q1 were delivered in under three hours.

Walmart Q1 FY27 earnings call transcript

This is what a mature omnichannel engine looks like when the parts start compounding together. Traffic, fulfillment, marketplace assortment, ad monetization, and membership are no longer separate workstreams. They are a single economic system. That raises the competitive bar for every brand that still treats digital commerce, CRM, and fulfillment as disconnected functions.

Use Walmart’s stack as a benchmark, not because the model is directly copyable, but because it exposes the right questions: Are members clearly more valuable than non-members, are marketplace partners funding demand through retail media, and is fulfillment speed materially improving conversion and repeat rate?

Target’s AI comments are a useful reality check for retail operating models

The signal is that AI adoption is now running into cost architecture, not just experimentation

Reuters reported on May 25 that Target is moving from “using AI to running on AI,” but Target India President Andrea Zimmerman said the shift to usage-based pricing is forcing the retailer to re-evaluate how broadly expensive AI tools should be made available across employees.

Reuters via Investing.com on Target AI tool costs

Reuters also reported that discussions about AI pricing are happening at the highest levels of Target’s architecture and technology leadership forums, as the company weighs trade-offs between employee demand and cost discipline.

Reuters relay on Target AI strategy reassessment

This matters because it cuts through the usual retail AI theater. Large retailers are discovering that broad AI deployment is not just a product question. It is a budgeting, access-control, and operating-model question. If Target is already tightening around unit economics, most retailers should assume their own AI rollout plans are probably too loose.

Treat AI like a portfolio of workflows, not a universal entitlement. Prioritize the use cases with measurable margin, labor, conversion, or speed impact, then design access and vendor economics around those workflows first.

Evidence caveat: this section is based on Reuters reporting relayed by third-party publishers, not a direct Target filing or company press release. The facts are attributable, but the sourcing is still second-hand.

Watch item for footwear wholesale this morning: DICK’S first-quarter results

This is one of the cleaner near-term reads on U.S. athletic demand and premium footwear shelf productivity

DICK’S Sporting Goods said on May 4 that it plans to report first-quarter fiscal 2026 results before the market opens on May 27, followed by an 8:00 a.m. Eastern conference call.

DICK’S Sporting Goods first-quarter results call scheduled for May 27, 2026

In that same release, DICK’S pointed investors to continued growth in House of Sport and Golf Galaxy Performance Center concepts as part of its ongoing strategic narrative.

DICK’S Sporting Goods first-quarter results call scheduled for May 27, 2026

For footwear brands selling through U.S. wholesale, this report matters less for the headline comp and more for what it says about premium athletic demand, experiential-store productivity, and how much appetite remains for higher-ticket performance product in a still-uneven consumer environment.

Watch for three things when the release lands: footwear comp strength versus general merchandise, any commentary on inventory and promotions, and whether premium performance continues to outperform the broader assortment.

Overall evidence quality is mixed today. Walmart is supported by primary company materials and is strong. The Target AI item is strategically useful but second-hand.
